ARC-AGI2 is the benchmark designed to resist brute-force AI approaches — Gemini 3 Deep Think now hits average human performance. Reasoning and reliability are improving rapidly and predictably — they're not the bottleneck.

Only Two Fundamental Limitations

1. Long-term Context Management

  • No true memory across sessions

2. Online Learning

  • Can't learn from experience — every session is a blank slate

Everything else is tractable engineering — moving at extraordinary speed.

These two may ultimately be solved by the same breakthrough. Both are active research areas. Engineering workarounds (compaction, memory files, RAG) are already shrinking the context discontinuity fast. Everything else — tool access, safety, parallelism, persistence — is tractable engineering that doesn't require fundamental research. The following slides show what that engineering looks like.

MCP & Skills

The foundations of agentic AI

MCP

  • Open standard: AI ↔ tools & data

Skills

  • Open standard: AI workflows & playbooks
MCP is the open standard that lets AI connect to any tool or data source — now adopted everywhere. Skills are the complementary standard: they encode what to do (workflows, playbooks), while MCP encodes how to connect. Both are central to the Claws architecture we'll see shortly. Also progressing rapidly: parallel agent teams (Anthropic ran 5 parallel Claude agents to build a C compiler, coordinating on a shared codebase), context compaction, and permission hooks. This is delegation and orchestration, not autocomplete.

Claude Cowork

Research preview — January 2026

  • Reads, edits, creates files · parallel tasks

Coworker, not chatbot.

Key insight: the same patterns that transformed coding apply to all knowledge work. Cowork is Claude Code for non-developers — you assign work and review results, not chat back and forth. This is where the agent pattern starts reaching business professionals, not just engineers. The interaction model shifts from "chatbot" to "coworker."

Claws 🦞

Karpathy — February 2026

LLMs → LLM Agents → Claws

  • Persistent AI orchestrator — calendar, email, browser, messaging
  • Built on MCP & Skills

OpenClaw

  • ~400K lines · broad integrations

NanoClaw

  • ~4K lines · "fits in my head"
Karpathy's layering model: LLMs → LLM Agents → Claws. A Claw is a locally-hosted AI assistant wired to your tools — calendar, email, browser, messaging. Not a chat window but a persistent orchestrator. Full Karpathy quote: "Claws are now a new layer on top of LLM agents, taking the orchestration, scheduling, context, tool calls and a kind of persistence to a next level." OpenClaw is the full-featured version (~400K lines, broad integrations, moving to open-source foundation). NanoClaw is Karpathy's minimalist alternative — ~4K lines, designed to be fully understandable by both humans and AI agents. This is where MCP and Skills become critical infrastructure.

This is a key observation: AI agents work well for code because code is structured and verifiable — tests, compilers, type checkers. Non-coding knowledge work — emails, documents, planning — lacks this. But AI itself will close this gap. Using its own software engineering capabilities, AI will accelerate digital transformation — structuring previously unstructured work, making it API-addressable and testable. This is putting on steroids a trend that's been running for decades. The question of whether AI handles "fuzzy" work well becomes less relevant as less work remains fuzzy. For genuine edge cases, AI systems can escalate — including spawning human contractors via platforms like Upwork.

AI Workers, Not AI Assistants

  • AI workers with their own accounts — email, calendar, tools
  • Assign work, review output — like a human colleague
  • OpenAI Frontier (Feb 2026) — Uber, Intuit, State Farm

The plumbing is simple. The cultural shift is the bigger part.

👤 You
assign & share ↓
🤖 AI Worker
📧 Own email · 📅 Own calendar
🔑 Scoped access
↑ reports & escalates
Own identity, bounded permissions
The Claws approach raises a security question — giving AI persistent access to your personal credentials is inherently exposed. A more natural evolution: long-lived AI workers with their own accounts, their own email, calendar, project tools. You share specific items as you would with a human colleague. OpenAI Frontier, launched Feb 2026, is already building exactly this — an enterprise platform to deploy AI agents as workers with onboarding, shared business context, and permissions. Uber, Intuit, State Farm among the first customers. BCG, McKinsey, Accenture, Capgemini are Frontier Alliance partners deploying it. The METR trajectory grounds this: doubling every ~123 days means week-long autonomous tasks are plausible by late 2026. Accounts tagged as AI, linked to human principals, clear escalation paths. We're already seeing this in customer service. The organizational plumbing is simple; the cultural adjustment is the bigger shift.

The Landscape Shift

EraInteractionCore Skill
Chat (2023)Prompt → ResponsePrompt engineering
Agents (2024–25)Delegate → ReviewTask decomposition, context engineering
Orchestration (2026+)Configure → OverseeSystems thinking, trust calibration
The skillset is shifting. Prompt engineering was 2023's core skill. Now it's about understanding tool ecosystems (MCP, Skills), agent coordination, and context engineering. By 2026+, it's systems thinking and trust calibration — knowing when and how much to delegate. Steve Yegge's framing captures it well: 2024 was the year of the Chatbot, 2025 the year of the Agent, 2026 is the year of Orchestration. His Gas Town project runs 20-30 parallel Claude Code instances organized into hierarchical colonies.
